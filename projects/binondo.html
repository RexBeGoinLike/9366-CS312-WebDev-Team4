Binondo–Intramuros Bridge Completed

By TransparencyPH Staff | Published April 5, 2022 | Updated 2025

The Binondo–Intramuros Bridge is a 680-meter, 4-lane cable-stayed crossing spanning the Pasig River, linking the historic walled city of Intramuros with Manila’s Chinatown in Binondo. Inaugurated on April 5, 2022, the bridge forms part of a broader effort to ease traffic congestion in central Manila and improve connectivity between commercial and cultural districts.

Project Information

  • Location: Manila, Metro Manila
  • Status: Completed (April 5, 2022)
  • Contractor: China Road and Bridge Corporation
  • Budget: ₱3.39 billion
  • Timeline: 2018–2022
  • Design: Cable-stayed, 4 lanes, pedestrian walkways planned
  • Primary goal: Modal decongestion, heritage area linkage

Background & Rationale

The bridge originated under the national infrastructure initiative known as “Build, Build, Build” and was financed through a grant linked to bilateral cooperation. Its proponents argued it would reduce central Manila traffic pressure, improve logistic flows to nearby ports, and spur micro economic activity along both riverbanks.

Heritage groups voiced concerns about visual and physical impacts to Intramuros, prompting heritage impact assessments and design tweaks during the planning phase. Authorities committed to mitigation measures such as context-sensitive lighting and landscaping.

Construction Highlights & Challenges

Construction involved riverbed piling, marine traffic management, and installation of cable stays and pylons set back from key heritage sightlines. The pandemic required adaptive workforce measures and supply-chain alternatives to meet milestones. Noise, temporary access restrictions, and traffic rearrangements were managed through stakeholder engagement programs.

Economic & Social Impact

Post-opening surveys and traffic counts indicate:

  • Average travel time between Binondo and Intramuros reduced to under 5 minutes during off-peak, with significant peak-hour relief for adjacent crossings.
  • Increased foot traffic benefiting small businesses along the approach roads, boosting retail and foodservice revenues.
  • Improved bus routing and logistics access for suppliers serving Chinatown markets.

Heritage & Environmental Considerations

Conservationists remain watchful: Intramuros stakeholders monitor visual integrity and air quality effects. The DPWH and local government established a monitoring committee to ensure mitigation measures—noise barriers, light-sensitive fixtures, and riverbank greening—are sustained.

Looking Ahead

Authorities are studying non-motorized transport options (protected bike lanes, widened sidewalks) to maximize public value. The bridge is also being considered as a tourist route in heritage walking circuits, linking cultural nodes with clearer, safer crossings.
